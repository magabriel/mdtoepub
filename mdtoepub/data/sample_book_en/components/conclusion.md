# Conclusion

Ashford closed the notebook and gazed out the train window. The orchards passed before his eyes like green stains against the ochre earth of the West Country. He carried with him a copy of the star map, Hargrove's diary, and the certainty that some questions were not meant to be answered.

Or perhaps they were — but not by him.

The commission from the City Council was over. The warehouse would be demolished in the spring, and with it the last traces of that textile manufactory which, without knowing it, had guarded for decades the thread of a far greater story.

But Ashford had decided not to let it be lost entirely.

He would begin to write. Not a report, not an academic paper. Something different. Something that gathered not only the facts, but the wonder of having discovered them.

The world was changing. New methods of <span class="sans">documentary classification</span> and <span class="sans">photographic reproduction</span> techniques would allow future generations to access materials that until then had been within reach of only a handful of scholars. The archivist's craft, that <span class="muted">silent sentinel of memory</span>, was beginning to be recognised as an essential piece in the preservation of heritage.

—

The final words of Hargrove's diary, written the night before his disappearance, summed up with simplicity the paradox of all historical research:

> *We have found what no one sought, but we do not know what we have found.*
