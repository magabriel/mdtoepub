*London, 12 March 1897*

The rain beat against the warehouse windows with the persistence of one who has nowhere to be. Edward W. Ashford raised his eyes from the notebook and peered through the fog that clouded the windowpane. Below, the Thames ran grey and choppy, carrying with it the detritus of the city that was stretching itself awake on the opposite bank.

He had been cataloguing the contents of that warehouse for three weeks, a commission he had accepted more out of necessity than interest. The preliminary list of materials was overwhelming:

> **Preliminary Inventory — Municipal Warehouse No. 7**
>
> 1. Commercial invoices of the *Royal Textile Manufactory* (1873–1881)[^1] — 14 bundles
> 2. Correspondence of the *Portsmouth Literary Society* (1865–1879)[^2] — 8 boxes
> 3. Public works blueprints of the city council (1840–1885) — 22 rolls
> 4. <del>Parish registers</del> (misplaced prior to transfer)
> 5. Miscellaneous papers unclassified — 5 boxes
> 6. A linen envelope with a <mark>red wax seal</mark> (*uncatalogued*)
> {.list-unstyled}

The professor had attempted to establish a systematic classification of the material following the method his mentor had taught him at Oxford:

Type of document
:   Classification according to its nature and origin.
Date
:   Certain or estimated date of composition.
State of preservation
:   Good, fair, deteriorated, or critical.
Topographical signature
:   Physical location within the archive.

—An archivist's work —he murmured to himself— tedious and poorly paid.

Ashford stoked the coal of the stove and rubbed his hands. The March cold cut to the bone in that uninsulated warehouse. He was thirty-four years old, held a junior lectureship at the Polytechnic Academy, and possessed a vocation for history that seldom found room to exercise itself. This commission was, in truth, the closest thing to research he had done in the past two years.

The afternoon advanced without incident. Towards five o'clock, when the light began to wane and Ashford was contemplating calling it a day, his fingers stumbled upon something different. At the bottom of a box marked `Miscellaneous papers — 1880–1883`, among receipts from an ironmonger's and invoices from a carpenter's workshop, there was a thick linen envelope, yellowish, sealed with a red wax stamp.

It bore neither sender nor addressee.

Ashford held it in his hands, weighing it. In the inventory notebook, he noted in a neat hand:

```
Date of discovery: 12 March 1897
Location: Box No. 14 (labelled "Miscellaneous papers — 1880–1883")
Description: Linen envelope, 21 x 13 cm, red wax seal intact
Observations: No sender or addressee marks. Wax bearing
  ring imprint: unidentified heraldic motif.
Condition: Good
```

Something in the texture of the paper, in the resistance of the wax, told him this was not a mislaid administrative document. It was something else. Something that had been waiting, perhaps for years, for someone to find it.

[^1]: The Royal Textile Manufactory of Portsmouth, founded in 1758, was one of the first industrial centres in southern England. Its archives were dispersed following its closure in 1881.

[^2]: The Portsmouth Literary Society, active between 1865 and 1879, brought together intellectuals and writers from the southern region. Its archive was donated to the Portsmouth City Council in 1880.
