England, last third of the nineteenth century. The country is living through times of change. Republican ideas germinate in the universities and coffeehouses of the great cities, the railway begins to weave a network that connects the territory, and iron-and-glass architecture starts to contest the skyline of the old brick facades.
{.lead}

But beneath the surface of this England peering toward modernity, far older strata persist. Remnants of a past that refuses to disappear:

- **Roman inscriptions** reused in medieval walls
- **Celtic legends** that survive in place names
- **Forgotten manuscripts** in monastic libraries
- **Star maps** whose meaning has been lost to the centuries

It is in this England of contrasts that *The Vestige of Centuries* unfolds. A story about what remains when everything seems to have vanished, about the invisible threads that connect distant eras, and about those who, willingly or not, become guardians of that memory.

> The past is not dead. It is not even past.
> — William Faulkner
