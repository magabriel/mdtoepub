*Oxford, 3 April 1897*

The envelope remained unopened in the drawer of Ashford's desk for three weeks. It was not superstition, nor even prudence: it was simply that he had not found the right moment. Lectures at the Academy, the marking of examinations, and the final days of cataloguing at the warehouse had occupied every hour of his days.

But that morning, with the light entering obliquely through the study window and his coffee steaming at his side, he decided the moment had arrived.

<span class="all-caps">Breaking the wax seal</span> was easier than he had expected. The wax, dry and brittle, came away in irregular fragments. Inside the envelope was a single sheet of laid paper, folded in four, covered in a tight, neat hand.

The letter read:

> *Dear Sir,*
>
> *The undersigned, lay brother of the Monastery of St. Benedict of Aviz-Maramonte[^1], writes this missive to leave record of a discovery which, by its nature, ought not to remain hidden any longer. In the course of restoration works on the high chapel of our church, ordered by His Grace the Bishop of Wells, there has been found, behind the high altar, a sealed cavity containing various objects whose origin and purpose we cannot fathom.*
>
> *Among them, a codex of small format stands out, bound in what appears to be goatskin, bearing inscriptions in characters that none of the brothers has managed to decipher. Accompanying the codex are several documents on parchment, equally illegible, as well as a set of pieces that appear to be fragments of a map or diagram.*
>
> *Given the unusual nature of the discovery, and the inability of the local ecclesiastical authorities to offer a satisfactory explanation, I have taken the liberty of sending this news to one who, I am given to understand, devotes himself to the study of antiquities.*
>
> *I beg of you to exercise the utmost discretion regarding this matter.*
>
> *I remain, your obedient servant,*
>
> *Brother Jerome of St. James*
> *Monastery of St. Benedict of Aviz-Maramonte*
> *15 February 1897*

The letter was not addressed to him. It bore no addressee's name. Yet it had reached his hands.

<span class="small-caps">Ashford</span> read the letter three times, and upon finishing the third reading, he knew that his life had just changed.

—This is no coincidence —he said aloud, and the sound of his own voice in the empty room struck him as strange.

He rose and began to pace, letter in hand. The name of Hargrove, the professor who had vanished in 1888[^2], was vaguely familiar to him. He recalled having read something in the Academy's annals, a footnote in a study on British diplomatic correspondence.

Three weeks later, Ashford would take the coach to Wells. He carried in his pocket the letter from Brother Jerome, a notebook, and the hunch that the warehouse discovery was but the first piece of a far greater puzzle.

> There is no discovery so small that it cannot alter the course
> of a life. It only takes someone who knows how to see it.
> {.pull-quote}

[^1]: The Monastery of St. Benedict of Aviz-Maramonte, founded in the thirteenth century, was one of the principal monastic centres of the Order of Aviz in the West Country. Its historical archive is currently preserved in the Wells Cathedral Library.

[^2]: The case of Dr. Edmund Hargrove, Professor at the University of Oxford who vanished in November 1888, was recorded by J. M. Thornton in *Tragic Events in Victorian London* (London, 1892).
