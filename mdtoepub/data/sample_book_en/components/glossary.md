# Glossary

Year of 1888
:   The date of Dr. Edmund Hargrove's disappearance, whose investigation triggers the events of the novel.

Archivist
:   A person entrusted with the custody, preservation, and cataloguing of documents. A traditionally silent craft, yet indispensable for the transmission of written memory.

Codex
:   A handwritten book predating the invention of printing. Codices were typically made on <span class="sans">parchment</span> or <span class="sans">vellum</span> and bound in wood covered with leather.

Cryptography
:   The art of writing in secret code. The sixteenth-century manual found in Bath employed a combination of <span class="small-caps">substitution</span> and <span class="small-caps">transposition</span>.

Diplomatics
:   The science that studies the structure, authenticity, and dating of historical documents. Not to be confused with <span class="muted">political diplomacy</span>.

Epigraphy
:   The study of ancient inscriptions on stone, metal, or other durable materials.

Incunable
:   A book printed in the fifteenth century, before 1501. The term derives from the Latin *incunabula* ('cradle').

Star map
:   A diagram of correspondences between celestial bodies and terrestrial events. The one found by Ashford and Cross was not a conventional astronomical map, but a <span class="sans">system of symbolic coordinates</span>.

Palimpsest
:   A manuscript whose original text has been erased to write another on top. A metaphor for history itself, where successive layers of writing conceal and reveal messages from the past.

Palaeography
:   The study of ancient writing and its evolution through time.

Sigilum
:   A seal or graphic sign used to authenticate documents. The word appears in the note found in Hargrove's pocket after his death.

**Note:** Terms marked with <abbr title="Abbreviations used in the cataloguing process">abbr.</abbr> correspond to technical vocabulary specific to archival science and the documentary disciplines.
