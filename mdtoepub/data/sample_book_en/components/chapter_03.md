*Edinburgh, 10 May 1898*

Ashford's first visit to the Monastery of St. Benedict of Aviz-Maramonte had been a fiasco.

The journey from Oxford to the far reaches of the West Country had taken him nearly two days by coach and hired cart. When at last he reached the monastery — a brown, silent bulk perched on a hill of cork oaks and stone — he was met with the news that Brother Jerome had died two months prior.

—Pneumonia —the brother porter told him, in the neutral tone of one announcing the weather—. We buried him at the beginning of March.

The restoration works on the chapel, for their part, had been completed. The high altar was back in place, and the sealed cavity had been resealed following the bishop's instructions.

—Nothing remains —the porter added—. Everything that was found was sent to London, to the National Archives, I believe.

But the National Archives had no record of any such delivery. Ashford spent three days reviewing registers, questioning archivists, following leads that dissolved like smoke. In the end, a veteran employee, taking pity on his frustration, confided a rumour to him:

—They say the shipment arrived, but that someone diverted it before it was registered. If you want my opinion, ask at the Royal Academy of Sciences. They have their own channels.

It took Ashford a year to pick up the correct trail. A year of letters, of fruitless visits, of broken promises. Until, in the spring of 1898, a chance conversation in the National Library of Scotland put him on the scent of Professor Raymond Cross, a historian at the University of Edinburgh who, it was said, was working on a manuscript found under mysterious circumstances.
