# Appendix: Historical Notes

## The Monastery of St. Benedict of Aviz-Maramonte

The monastery was founded in the <span class="small-caps">thirteenth</span> century by the Order of Aviz[^1], one of the English military-religious orders. Its church, in the Gothic style, was extensively remodelled in the <span class="small-caps">eighteenth</span> century, during which period the Baroque high altarpiece that would play a central role in the discovery narrated in this novel was added. The monastery was designated a listed building in 1910 and currently houses a museum of sacred art.

## Cryptography in Sixteenth-Century England

The cryptography manual that appears in the novel is inspired by real works such as the *Polygraphiae* of Trithemius[^2] (1518) and the *De cifris* of Alberti (1466). It is known that the English court of the <span class="small-caps">sixteenth</span> century maintained encoded correspondence with its ambassadors throughout Europe, and that there existed at the University of Oxford a chair of *secret arts* that included the teaching of cryptographic techniques, although the records of that chair are fragmentary.

The encryption methods employed in the period included:

1. **Simple substitution** — each letter is replaced by another sign
2. **Transposition** — letters are reordered according to a key
3. **Polyalphabetic cipher** — use of multiple substitution alphabets
4. **Steganography** — concealment of the message within an innocuous appearance

## Radiocarbon Dating Techniques

The laboratory at the University of Oxford subjected the parchments to <sup>14</sup>C dating, yielding a date range of 1470–1520 AD with a 95% margin of error. The figure coincided with the reign of Henry VII (r. 1485–1509), a period of cultural splendour known as the *Tudor Renaissance*.

The calibration formula used was:

> t = -8033 &middot; ln(F<sub>m</sub>)

where F<sub>m</sub> represents the fraction of <sup>14</sup>C measured relative to the modern standard.
