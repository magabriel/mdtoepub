*Cambridge, 2 January 1899*

The Star Map[^1] was not a celestial map, despite its name. It was a diagram of correspondences, a labyrinth of lines and symbols connecting points on a surface of yellowed parchment. Ashford and Cross[^2] had spent six months deciphering the first keys of the codex using the manual from Bath, and the result was that map.

But they could not fathom what it represented.

—These are not geographical coordinates —said Cross, bending over the table where they had spread the facsimile—. The distances do not match.

—Perhaps they are not physical distances —Ashford suggested.

Cross raised his eyes, and for a long moment their gazes met above the parchment.

—What do you mean?

—Look at these marks —Ashford pointed to several points on the diagram—. They are not cities. They are dates. Or rather: events. The author of the codex was not drawing a map of places. He was drawing a map of happenings. A sequence.

Cross fell silent, tracing the lines of the diagram with his finger.

—If you are right —he said at last— then what we are searching for is not a treasure. It is a story. A story that someone cut into pieces and scattered through time so that it could only be read in the correct order.

—And if someone has been reading it before us —Ashford added— if the pieces have already been reassembled...

Cross nodded slowly.

—Then perhaps we are not the first to arrive. But perhaps we may be the first to understand what it means.

Outside, the January wind lashed the cobbled streets of Cambridge, and in the silence of the study, the two men bent once more over the parchment, striving to decipher the message that someone, centuries before, had woven into that labyrinth of ink and vellum.

[^1]: The concept of a "star map" as a system of symbolic coordinates has historical precedents in the Hermetic-Renaissance tradition. The manuscript *Mappa Stellae* of Athanasius Kircher (1665) describes a similar system of correspondences between stars and events.

[^2]: Raymond Cross (1844–1912) was a British historian and palaeographer, author of *Studies in English Palaeography* (Oxford, 1887) and Fellow of the Royal Society of London.
