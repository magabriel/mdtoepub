Without attention there is no learning. This statement, which may seem like a truism, has profound implications that we often overlook. Attention is not simply "paying attention": it is a cognitive resource that is limited, fragile, and extraordinarily valuable.

> Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity.
> — Simone Weil

To understand why attention is so crucial, let us begin with a thought experiment.

## The invisible gorilla experiment

In 1999, psychologists Daniel Simons and Christopher Chabris[^1] conducted an experiment that has become a classic of cognitive psychology. They asked participants to watch a video in which two teams passed a basketball back and forth. The task was to count the number of passes made by the team in white.

Halfway through the video, a person dressed as a gorilla walked slowly across the scene, stopped in the middle, beat their chest, and walked on. The question: how many participants saw the gorilla?

The answer: **more than half did not see it**. They were so focused on counting passes that the gorila went entirely unnoticed.

**Practical application:** When you study while looking at your phone, listening to music with lyrics, or have multiple tabs open, you are creating your own "invisible gorilla": important content passes before your eyes without you truly processing it.
{.tip}

## The myths of multitasking

Most people believe they can do several things at once. Science says otherwise:

Myth
:   Doing several tasks at once makes me more productive.
Reality
:   Constantly switching tasks reduces performance by up to 40%.
{.definition}

Myth
:   I am good at multitasking.
Reality
:   People who believe they are better at multitasking tend to perform worse at it.
{.definition}

**Warning:** Multitasking does not really exist. What we call multitasking is actually rapid and constant switching of attention between tasks. Each time you switch, you pay a "switching cost" that reduces your efficiency and increases the likelihood of errors.
{.warning}

## Strategies for improving attention

Below are the most effective strategies backed by research:

### 1. The Pomodoro technique

Developed by Francesco Cirillo[^2] in the 1980s, this technique consists of working in focused time blocks interspersed with short breaks:

![The Pomodoro technique cycle](images/illustrations/pomodoro.png)

```
Pomodoro 1: 25 min work + 5 min break
Pomodoro 2: 25 min work + 5 min break
Pomodoro 3: 25 min work + 5 min break
Pomodoro 4: 25 min work + 15 min long break
```

**Example:** Maria used to study for hours without rest and noticed that after 30 minutes her mind began to wander. By applying the Pomodoro technique, she managed to maintain concentration for the full 25 minutes, knowing that a break would come soon. After a week, her academic performance had noticeably improved.
{.example}

### 2. The optimal environment

Your physical environment has a direct impact on your ability to pay attention:

- **Lighting:** Natural light whenever possible
- **Noise:** Silence or white noise. Avoid music with lyrics
- **Order:** A tidy space reduces cognitive load
- **Digital:** Phone in another room or on aeroplane mode

**Note:** Research shows that even the mere presence of the phone on the desk — even if it is switched off — reduces available cognitive capacity. Literally, your brain spends resources on not looking at it.

### 3. Single-focus approach

When you begin a study session, commit to **one single task** and pledge not to switch until the break.

**Exercise: Schedule your attention**
{.exercise}

For one week, note down how you spend each hour of your day. At the end of the week, identify:

1. How many hours did you devote to deep work (without interruptions)?
2. How many times did you switch tasks in one hour?
3. At what times of day are you most productive?
