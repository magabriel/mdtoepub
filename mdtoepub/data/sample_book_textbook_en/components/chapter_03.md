We have seen *how* to pay attention and *how* to remember. But there is a factor that determines whether you will actually put those techniques into practice: your **mindset**.

> Whether you think you can or you think you can't, you're right.
> — Henry Ford

Mindset is the filter through which you interpret your learning experiences. It determines how you face failure, how you respond to difficulty, and ultimately, how far you go in your development.
{.lead}

## Fixed mindset vs. growth mindset

Psychologist Carol Dweck[^1], of Stanford University, spent decades studying how our beliefs about intelligence affect our performance. She identified two profiles:

![Comparative diagram of fixed and growth mindsets](images/illustrations/mindmap.png)

**Fixed Mindset**
:   Belief that intelligence and abilities are fixed, immutable traits. People with this mindset avoid challenges, give up easily, and see effort as pointless.
{.definition}

**Growth Mindset**
:   Belief that intelligence and abilities can be developed through effort, practice, and learning. People with this mindset embrace challenges, persist in the face of obstacles, and see effort as the path to mastery.
{.definition}

**Self-assessment: Which mindset do you have?** The next time you face a difficult task, listen to your self-talk. Think: "This is too hard for me" (fixed) or "I can't do this *yet*, but I can learn" (growth). The simple act of identifying patterns is the first step to changing them.
{.tip}

## The power of "yet"

One of the simplest and most powerful interventions that Dweck and her team developed consists of adding a single word: **yet**.

> I don't understand this. -> I don't understand this **yet**.
> I'm not good at maths. -> I'm not good at maths **yet**.
> I can't code. -> I can't code **yet**.

**Case study:** An experiment with secondary school students divided participants into two groups. Group A was taught that intelligence is malleable. Group B was given no information. After one semester, Group A's grades had improved significantly compared to Group B's, especially in mathematics.
{.example}

## The role of failure

**Counterintuitive idea:** Failure is not the enemy of learning. It is an essential part of the process. Every error is a signal your brain uses to adjust its predictions and improve.
{.warning}

**Practical example:** When you learn to ride a bicycle, you fall. Many times. But each fall teaches your brain something new about balance, speed, and coordination. Without falls, you do not learn. The same applies to any cognitive skill.
{.example}

The key is not to avoid failure, but to:

1. **Interpret it correctly:** It is not "I am bad at this", but "that did not work; what can I change?"
2. **Extract the lesson:** Every error contains valuable information
3. **Adjust and try again:** Learning is iterative

> Success is going from failure to failure without losing enthusiasm.
> — Winston Churchill
> {.pull-quote}

## Strategies for developing a growth mindset

Below are four practical strategies backed by research:

### 1. Reframe your self-talk

When you catch yourself saying "I can't", add the word "yet". When you think "this is too hard", ask yourself: "What concrete step could I take to make progress?"

### 2. Celebrate effort, not just results

Instead of congratulating yourself for passing the exam, celebrate having studied with a method, for having strived to understand the concepts, for having persisted when things got difficult.

**Note:** This is especially important if you have children or students. The way you praise shapes their mindset. Praise the process ("I like how hard you worked"), not the person ("how clever you are").
{.note}

### 3. Deliberately seek difficulty

Most people avoid what is hard. You do the opposite: actively seek problems that are one step beyond your current level.

**Zone of Proximal Development (Vygotsky)[^2]**
:   The distance between what you can do on your own and what you can do with help. Optimal learning occurs when you work in this zone: difficult enough to stretch you, but not so much as to frustrate you.
{.definition}

### 4. Keep a learning journal

At the end of each day, write down three things:

1. What did I learn today?
2. What did I find difficult?
3. How am I going to apply what I learned?

**Exercise: The learner's journal**
{.exercise}

For the next two weeks, spend five minutes each evening writing your learning journal. After two weeks, re-read what you have written and observe the patterns: in which areas have you improved? What challenges keep recurring?

## Chapter conclusion

A growth mindset is not something you either have or do not have. It is a practice, a decision made each day, each time you face a challenge.

[^1]: Dweck, C. S. (2006). *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*. Random House. Dweck's research has been replicated in multiple educational and cultural contexts, demonstrating the robustness of her findings.

[^2]: Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). *Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes*. Harvard University Press. Vygotsky proposed that optimal learning occurs when the student works with tasks they cannot do alone but can do with the help of a more competent guide.

**Key ideas from this chapter**
{.key-takeaway}

- Your beliefs about intelligence affect your actual performance
- Adding the word "yet" changes your perspective
- Failure is a source of information, not a verdict
- Actively seek difficulty: that is where learning grows
- Keep a record of your process to see your progress
