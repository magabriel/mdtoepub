# Notes

The following notes provide additional context and references for claims made throughout the text.

## Chapter 1

[^1]: Simons, D. J., and Chabris, C. F. (1999). Gorillas in our midst: Sustained inattentional blindness for dynamic events. *Perception*, 28(9), 1059-1074. This landmark study has been replicated numerous times across different cultures and age groups, consistently demonstrating that approximately 50% of observers fail to notice unexpected events when focused on a primary task.

[^2]: Cirillo, F. (2006). *The Pomodoro Technique*. Creative Commons. Francesco Cirillo developed the technique in the late 1980s while a university student. The name derives from the tomato-shaped kitchen timer he used.

[^3]: Ward, A. F., Duke, K., Gneezy, A., and Bos, M. W. (2017). Brain drain: The mere presence of one's own smartphone reduces available cognitive capacity. *Journal of the Association for Consumer Research*, 2(2), 140-154.

## Chapter 2

[^4]: Ebbinghaus, H. (1885). *Uber das Gedachtnis* (*On Memory*). Leipzig: Duncker and Humblot. Ebbinghaus served as his own subject, memorising lists of nonsense syllables and testing himself at various intervals.

[^5]: Roediger, H. L., and Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. *Psychological Science*, 17(3), 249-255. Students who tested themselves remembered significantly more material one week later than students who simply re-read the same material.

[^6]: Kornell, N., and Bjork, R. A. (2008). Learning concepts and categories: Is spacing the "enemy of induction"? *Psychological Science*, 19(6), 585-592. Interleaved practice feels harder but produces superior long-term learning.

## Chapter 3

[^7]: Dweck, C. S. (2006). *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*. Random House. Dweck's research has been conducted across diverse populations, including students in the United States, East Asia, and Europe.

[^8]: Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). *Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes*. Harvard University Press. Vygotsky's work, originally published in Russian, was not widely available in English until the late 1970s but has since become one of the most influential frameworks in educational psychology.

[^9]: Yeager, D. S., and Dweck, C. S. (2012). Mindsets that promote resilience: When students believe that personal characteristics can be developed. *Educational Psychologist*, 47(4), 302-314.
