Hermann Ebbinghaus[^1] discovered something unsettling: we forget most of what we learn within hours. But he also discovered something hopeful: **each spaced review fixes the memory more firmly**.

This chapter will teach you how to build lasting knowledge.

Building solid knowledge is not a matter of repeating the same content over and over until it is branded into memory. It is a matter of *how* you repeat, *when* you repeat, and above all, *what* you do while repeating.
{.lead}

## The forgetting curve and spaced repetition

Ebbinghaus demonstrated that forgetting follows a predictable pattern:

![Ebbinghaus forgetting curve with spaced reviews](images/illustrations/learning_curve.png)

```
Retention (%)
100 | Learning point
 90 |
 80 |      * First review (24h)
 70 |      |
 60 |      *-------* Second review (7 days)
 50 |      |       |
 40 |      |       *-------* Third review (30 days)
 30 |      |       |       |
 20 |      |       |       *-----------
 10 |      |       |       |
  0 +------+-------+-------+----------
     Day 1 Day 2   Day 7   Day 30
```

**The golden rule:** Review new material at 24 hours, 7 days, and 30 days. Those three reviews are enough to fix the content in long-term memory.
{.tip}

## Deep encoding strategies

Not all review is equally effective. The key lies in **deep encoding**: processing information at levels of meaning, not just surface.

### 1. Active recall

It consists of closing the book and trying to remember the information by yourself. It is uncomfortably difficult — and that is why it works.

**Instead of:** "I am going to re-read this chapter again"
{.example}

**Do this:** Close the book and write down everything you remember on a sheet of paper. Then open the book and check what you missed.

Research shows that active recall is up to **50% more effective**[^2] than re-reading for the same amount of time invested.

<!-- Table: Comparative effectiveness of study strategies -->
| Strategy | Retention at 1 week | Retention at 1 month |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Re-reading | 28% | 15% |
| Highlighting | 30% | 18% |
| Active recall | 62% | 51% |
| Spaced practice + active | 78% | 68% |

**Note:** Re-reading and highlighting are the most popular strategies among students. They are also among the least effective. They give us a false sense of fluency ("I have read it, I know it") that prevents us from investing effort in more effective strategies.
{.note}

### 2. Elaboration

Explain the concept in your own words, as if you were teaching it to someone who knows nothing about the subject.

**Generation effect**
:   Phenomenon by which we remember better the information we have generated ourselves (for example, writing a summary in our own words) than information we have only read.
{.definition}

**Example of elaboration:**
{.example}

*Original concept:* "Working memory has a limited capacity of approximately 7 +/- 2 elements."

*Your elaboration:* "Working memory is like a small workbench. Only between 5 and 9 things can fit on it at once. If you put more, something falls off. That is why I cannot remember a long phone number without repeating it to myself."

### 3. Interleaved practice

Instead of practising a single type of problem until you master it (blocked practice), mix different types of problems in the same session.

**Caution:** Interleaved practice feels harder and more frustrating than blocked practice. Students often feel they are learning less. The data show the opposite: learning is deeper and more lasting.
{.warning}

### 4. The memory palace

One of the oldest and most powerful mnemonic techniques:

1. Choose a place you know well (your house, your neighbourhood)
2. Assign a specific location to each concept
3. Mentally walk through the place to retrieve the information

**To get started:** Use the rooms of your house as mental "shelves". The kitchen for basic concepts, the living room for intermediate concepts, the bedroom for advanced concepts. The stranger the visual association, the better you will remember it.
{.tip}

**Practical exercise: Test your memory**
{.exercise}

1. Read one page of any textbook
2. Close the book and write down everything you remember (active recall)
3. Open the book and add what was missing
4. Repeat the process 24 hours later

[^1]: Ebbinghaus, H. (1885). *Uber das Gedachtnis* (*On Memory*). Leipzig: Duncker and Humblot. Ebbinghaus was the first to apply the experimental method to the study of memory, using nonsense syllables to isolate the process of pure memorisation.

[^2]: Roediger, H. L., and Karpicke, J. D. (2006). Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention. *Psychological Science*, 17(3), 249-255.

**Key ideas from this chapter**
{.key-takeaway}

- Active recall is more effective than re-reading
- Space your reviews: 24h, 7 days, 30 days
- Explain in your own words (elaboration)
- Interleave different subjects in the same session
- Use mnemonic techniques for complex information
